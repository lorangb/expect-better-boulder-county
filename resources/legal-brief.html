The place & the baseline

A 1969 plant, frozen to its 1994 scope

  • The Lyons cement plant was built in 1969; the Boulder County Land Use Code was adopted in 1994, making the plant a legal nonconforming (grandfathered) use on ~866 acres now zoned Agricultural.
  • In the 1994 baseline, raw materials were supplied locally/on-site plus de minimis hauling from the Larimer County quarry — minimal Highway 66 truck traffic. (The Dowe Flats conveyor across the highway was approved June 1994 but not completed until 1997.)
  • Highway 66 (Ute Highway) is a prime tourist route to Estes Park and Rocky Mountain National Park, ~20 miles away.

The rule

A nonconforming use may continue as it existed in 1994 — but may not be enlarged or intensified. The legal question is always: how does today compare to 1994?

The governing standard

When a nonconforming use is terminated

Boulder County's Land Use Code terminates nonconforming status upon any —

"enlargement or alteration of the nonconforming use which has the effect … of creating a hazard or nuisance on or off the property, of adversely affecting the character of the neighborhood, or of intensifying the use of the land or its need for services."

The County applied exactly this standard in April 2024. The doubling of truck traffic is an enlargement/intensification — and a Highway 66 safety hazard — that forfeits the grandfathered status.

The centerpiece

The rescission rests on one claim — that today's traffic is no greater than 1994.

That claim is false, for three reasons the record makes plain:

1 · 1994 wasn't truck-fed

Raw materials were local/on-site — not trucked.

2 · Landis omits 1994

CEMEX's study hides the baseline it claims to match.

3 · The tonnage proves it

GNL's 1994 data can't support today's trucking.

1
What 1994 actually looked like

In 1994, the plant barely used Highway 66 for raw materials

  • In 1994, raw materials were supplied locally/on-site plus de minimis hauling from CEMEX's Larimer County quarry (M-1977-341). Public-road truck traffic was minimal.
  • The Dowe Flats conveyor across Highway 66 was approved in June 1994 but not completed until 1997 — so it wasn't even operating at the baseline, and it later moved material by belt, never by truck.
  • GNL's 1994 tonnage data — and the Boulder County Health Department annual reports for 1993 and 1994 (amicus Exhibits A & B) — document volumes that cannot equate to today's truck counts.

Never truck-fed — until 2022

From the 1994 baseline through 2022, raw materials came from local/on-site sources and then (after 1997) a conveyor — never by truck. Truck-hauling over Highway 66 is a 2022 change in how the plant operates.

Exhibits: GNL 1994 tonnage data; BoCo Health Dept. Annual Reports 1993 & 1994 (repo §08, 1994 Operational Baseline)Slide 9
2
CEMEX's evidence, examined

The Landis report omits 1994 — and doesn't add up

What CEMEX submitted

The Landis traffic report (Ex. D, Nov 2024) is CEMEX's "evidence of error" — the basis for the rescission. It argues current truck traffic is consistent with the historical, grandfathered operation.

The fatal gap

It omits the 1994 tonnage and traffic data entirely. You cannot prove "no increase since 1994" with a study that never shows 1994. GNL's tonnage data fills that gap — and refutes the conclusion.

Beyond hiding the baseline, GNL's review identifies factual errors, mathematical errors, and arbitrary statistical choices in the Landis report. A study this flawed — and missing 1994 entirely — cannot support reversing the County's determination.

Exhibits: Ex. D Landis Report; Ex. C Stantec Report; GNL rebuttal (repo §08, CEMEX_Responses)Slide 10
3
The increase is structural — and permanent

Why the trucks came: the quarry closed

  • When the County denied the Dowe Flats extension and the quarry closed in October 2022, the conveyor-fed local supply ended.
  • CEMEX now trucks raw materials in over Highway 66 — which is precisely why daily trips roughly doubled.
  • This is not "a continuation of historical truck traffic." It is a new, more intensive way of running the plant that did not exist in 1994 — and will not reverse.

A documented pattern of unpermitted intensification

The 1997–2000 expansion

A ~$10.3M "major modification" the EPA found increased NOx, particulates, and SO₂ — done without the required Clean Air Act permits.

The penalties

Federal consent decree $1M (2013) + SNCR controls; State of Colorado ~$600K (2009); CDPHE $357K (2023) and $1.39M (2024) for air-quality violations.

CEMEX has repeatedly grown and run the plant beyond its permits. The traffic surge is the latest chapter, not an aberration.

Independent support · C

The original use also changed character

  • The original Lyons quarry was wound down and reclaimed — "Disposal of Concrete Rubble and Demolition of Plant" (2003), with reclamation continuing through 2023.
  • Mining shifted to Dowe Flats (M-1993-041) — then ended in 2022. A plant once fed by an on-site, conveyed supply is now an import-and-truck operation.

The same tension

CEMEX cannot claim the 1994 use "never changed" to keep its protection while running a materially different, truck-fed operation today.

Precedent in our favor

Save Our St. Vrain Valley v. Boulder County BOA (2021)

The Court of Appeals held that what counts is activity directly related to the permitted use itself — not ancillary upkeep. Substance over paperwork.

  • Same valley, same Code, same community group — and a reversal for the citizens.
  • The Board of Adjustment is the proper forum to review the Director's determination — and to correct it when the law is misapplied, as it was in the rescission.

Anticipating CEMEX

The defenses — and the answers

"Sierra v. Billingsley"

That 2007 case was about whether a 1996 lapse rule applied to a 1990 tire-burning permit — not whether the use was unlawfully enlarged. It doesn't reach intensification.

"It's just historical truck traffic"

It isn't. The trucks replaced a conveyor that fed the plant in 1994. That's a new mode of operation — the definition of intensification.

"Closing us raises housing costs / kills jobs"

Real concerns — but not legal answers to enlargement of a nonconforming use. The Code question is intensification vs. 1994, full stop.

Sierra Club v. Billingsley, 166 P.3d 309 (Colo. App. 2007); CEMEX public statements (BizWest)Slide 16
The end state the rescission ignores

There is no lawful path back to a cement plant here

  • In March 2024 the County rezoned the parcel to Agricultural, where a cement plant is not a permitted use.
  • Director Case stated CEMEX could not even apply for a Special Use permit to continue operating.
  • So termination is the lawful end state. The rescission props up an industrial use that has no route to conformity — exactly what the doctrine is meant to wind down.

Why it matters

Reinstating the termination doesn't create a new burden — it restores the County's correct application of its own Code.

The decision before the Board

What the Board of Adjustment should do

1 · Reverse the rescission

Set aside the May 2026 rescission as resting on a study that omits the 1994 baseline.

2 · Reinstate the termination

Restore the April 2024 Notice of Termination — the County's correct finding of intensification.

3 · Apply the Code

Confirm the nonconforming use is terminated; with Agricultural zoning, there is no Special-Use path back.

Any one ground — the traffic intensification, the physical expansion, or the changed character of the use — is sufficient.
