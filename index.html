What Happened

Two Years of Progress. Reversed in a Single Sentence.

In April 2024, Boulder County agreed with the community that CEMEX had lost its grandfathered operating status. Two years later — eight days after the State of Colorado fined CEMEX $855,000 for air quality violations — the County's Planning Director reversed his own determination, with no new traffic data and no explanation. We appealed before the June 18, 2026 deadline. A public hearing before the Boulder County Board of Adjustment will now decide CEMEX's fate.

April 10, 2024
Boulder County Issues Notice of Termination
County Planning Director finds CEMEX violated Land Use Code Article 4-1003 — its grandfathered nonconforming use status was terminated (Case ZON-23-0003). CEMEX could continue operating during appeal.
May 19, 2026
Director Dale Case Reverses Himself
In a 3-page letter, the County's own Planning Director rescinds the Notice of Termination. The entire merits finding rests on a single sentence — claiming "no significant increase in truck traffic" since 1994. No new traffic data. No explanation. This came just 8 days after Colorado fined CEMEX $855,000.
Before June 18, 2026
GNL + SOSVV File Appeal
Good Neighbors of Lyons and Save Our Saint Vrain Valley file a formal appeal with the Boulder County Board of Adjustment — before the deadline — to contest the Director's rescission.
Now
Public BOA Hearing Ahead
A public hearing before the Boulder County Board of Adjustment will determine whether Boulder County will enforce its own rules. Date not yet announced — likely July or August 2026. Your voice matters.

The bottom line: Under Boulder County's own Land Use Code, the burden of proof was on CEMEX — not the community — to demonstrate its operations hadn't changed. Even CEMEX's own traffic study shows a 700%+ increase in truck trips compared to actual 1994 levels. Two years of work was undone in a single sentence.

The Argument

The Law Is on Our Side

You don't need a law degree to understand this. Boulder County adopted its Land Use Code in 1994. Plants like CEMEX that pre-dated the Code became "nonconforming uses" — commonly called grandfathered. They may continue, but the Code is explicit: they may not be enlarged, intensified, or changed in character.

Boulder County Land Use Code

What "nonconforming" means

When the 1994 Code was adopted, the Lyons cement plant became a legal nonconforming use. It was allowed to continue — but only as it existed then. The County has a stated duty under Article 4-1001.A to eliminate nonconformities "as speedily as justice will permit."

  • May continue — but only at the 1994 baseline of use.
  • May NOT be enlarged — no new structures intensifying the use.
  • May NOT be intensified — no significant traffic, emissions, or service-demand increase.
  • May NOT change character — the operation must remain the same kind of operation.
Article 4-1003.C.1.d — Termination Trigger

Three independent grounds for termination

  1. Traffic intensification — Truck trips rose from ~37 ADT (1994 baseline) to 303+ ADT in 2023 and 498–625 ADT in 2024–26 — a 700%+ increase, by CEMEX's own data.
  2. Physical expansion — Dozens of new structures built since 1994 without required permits: telecommunications towers on silos, dust collector systems, electrical upgrades, drilling wells, and signage (all documented in the New Structures evidence file).
  3. Changed character of use — A conveyor-fed quarry operation (limestone and shale moved by enclosed belt across Highway 66 from Dowe Flats) was transformed into a truck-import operation hauling raw materials from Wyoming ~250 miles away.

If any of these three grounds is proven, the right to continue the nonconforming use "terminates immediately." The County's burden-of-proof framework under the Code puts the onus on the property owner — not the community — to show the use hasn't changed.

Direct legal precedent: Save Our St. Vrain Valley v. Boulder County Board of Adjustment (Colo. App. 2021) — a Colorado Court of Appeals decision directly on point. The same community group, the same plant, the same Land Use Code, and the same Board of Adjustment. The Court affirmed that Boulder County's rules mean what they say: nonconforming uses cannot be enlarged or intensified. The 2021 ruling is the legal backbone of today's appeal.

If We Win

If the BOA reverses the rescission and reinstates the Notice of Termination, CEMEX's nonconforming use status is terminated. To continue operating, CEMEX would need to apply for a Special Use Permit — a public process with a 13-criteria vote. Under the Agricultural zoning now in place (the Commissioners rezoned the parcel in March 2024), Director Case has indicated CEMEX would not be able to apply. Cessation would trigger CEMEX's DRMS Reclamation Plan — currently estimated at $21M+.

"This is no longer just a fight about one company's compliance record. It is a fight about whether Boulder County will enforce its own rules. The hearing will be public. The record will include community voices. Yours matters."
