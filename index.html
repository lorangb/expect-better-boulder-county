An Open Letter

A Letter to the County That Saved Itself

Dear Boulder County,

We came here because we believed a different way was possible.

Decades ago, people moved to these foothills not for the views alone but for a conviction: that a community could live lightly on the land, keep its ridgelines wild, its creeks clear, its air sweet. They fought for every acre of open space when the smart money said it was impossible. They organized, they petitioned, they showed up. And they won.

Slowly, the open space program became a national model. The scientists came: PhDs, climate modelers, data analysts from every continent. They built NCAR. They wrote the papers the world quotes. Boulder County became proof that a place could grow without losing its soul.

We have money now. Tools. Data. Some of the smartest people on the planet live in our foothills.

And somewhere along the way, we started to believe the work was done.

So we forgot to look in our own backyard. CEMEX has been there the whole time: a coal-fired cement plant in Lyons, built in 1969, still running on air-quality standards from that decade. The same year we first walked on the moon, this plant started burning coal. In some ways, it never changed.

It is Boulder County's single largest source of CO2 emissions, 7% of all our climate pollution, from one smokestack. It has racked up $3.5 million in fines in four years and kept running. When it started hauling limestone from Wyoming, truck traffic through Lyons jumped 17x from roughly 37 trips a day to 625 and climbing. And 625 is just inbound Wyoming limestone. It does not count shale from Arcosa near Golden, gypsum, silica, or outbound finished cement shipments. The real number is meaningfully higher. Our neighbors sit in the dust. Our kids sit in the dust.

This is not a Republican issue or a Democratic issue. It is a Boulder County issue. Are we still the place we said we would be? Or have we become the kind of community that lets hard things slide?

The Board of Adjustment hearing is our moment. The scientists measured the carbon. The watchdogs tracked the fines. The traffic analysts counted the trucks. The historians pulled the records back to 1969. The attorneys built the case on three independent grounds under the Land Use Code. The courts have already spoken once, in Save Our St. Vrain Valley v. Boulder County BOA, 2021. We have the science, the law, the data, the precedent.

Now we need the people.

We need the same people who saved a valley, a trail, a ridgeline. The same people who passed the open space tax because they believed a different way was possible. We need you to write the letter, make the call, fill the room, stand at the microphone one more time.

This is not the hardest fight we have faced. We have faced worse, and we have won. This is simply the next one, waiting in our own backyard while we were busy being proud of everything we had already done.

Because if not us, who? And if not now, when?

Yours, in the old fire, The Good Neighbors of Lyons · Supported by Save Our Saint Vrain Valley (SOSVV), 501(c)(3)

The Legal Case

The Law is Clear. The Question is Whether We Enforce It.

A nonconforming use means CEMEX was grandfathered when Boulder County's Land Use Code took effect in 1994. The County allowed the plant to keep running, but the law says a nonconforming use cannot be enlarged, intensified, or changed in character. That is the deal. CEMEX broke the deal in three ways.

Traffic, 17x

From 37 ADT baseline to 625+ ADT (just Wyoming limestone) and climbing. And 625 is a floor: it excludes shale, gypsum, silica, and outbound shipments.

Structures, Dozens Added

New buildings, crushers, conveyors, and haul roads constructed without the use permits a conforming operation would require.

Character, Quarry → Truck-Import

The use shifted from a local quarry to a regional import-and-process operation. A change in character, not merely a change in volume.

How we got here

1994Boulder County Land Use Code adopted; CEMEX grandfathered as nonconforming use.
2021Save Our St. Vrain Valley v. Boulder County BOA, Court of Appeals rules for the community.
Apr 2024County staff find CEMEX in violation; the case moves forward.
May 2026The County reverses itself, with no explanation. The community appeals.
June 18, 2026Appeal filed before the statutory deadline. The case is live.
